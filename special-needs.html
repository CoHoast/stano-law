Planning for Loved Ones with Disabilities

When you have a family member with a disability, your estate planning takes on special importance. A well-intentioned inheritance can actually disqualify your loved one from essential government benefits like Medicaid and Supplemental Security Income (SSI).

Special needs planning ensures your disabled family member can receive an inheritance while maintaining eligibility for the benefits they depend on. The key is a properly drafted Special Needs Trust.

Why This Matters

A direct inheritance of just $2,000 can disqualify a disabled person from Medicaid and SSI. A Special Needs Trust allows them to benefit from your assets without losing these crucial benefits.

Types of Special Needs Trusts

Third-Party Trust

Created by parents, grandparents, or others to hold assets for a disabled beneficiary. No payback requirement to the government when the beneficiary passes.

First-Party Trust

Funded with the disabled person's own assets (inheritance, lawsuit settlement). Requires Medicaid payback upon death, but preserves benefits during lifetime.

Pooled Trust

Managed by nonprofit organizations, pooling resources from multiple beneficiaries. Good option for smaller amounts or when no family trustee is available.

ABLE Account

Tax-advantaged savings account for qualified disability expenses. Allows up to $100,000 without affecting SSI eligibility.

What Can a Special Needs Trust Pay For?

A Special Needs Trust can enhance your loved one's quality of life by paying for things government benefits don't cover:

  • Education and training — tutoring, vocational programs, college expenses
  • Transportation — vehicle purchase, maintenance, ride services
  • Personal care — additional caregivers, companions
  • Recreation — vacations, hobbies, entertainment, sports
  • Technology — computers, tablets, adaptive equipment
  • Housing improvements — accessibility modifications, furniture
  • Medical expenses — treatments not covered by Medicaid
