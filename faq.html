Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to common questions about elder law and estate planning

Medicaid Planning

What is the Medicaid look-back period in Ohio?

Ohio has a 5-year (60-month) look-back period for Medicaid applications. This means any asset transfers made within 5 years of applying for Medicaid can result in a penalty period during which you won't receive benefits. Planning ahead is crucial to maximize your options.

Will I lose my home if I go on Medicaid?

Not necessarily. Your home is generally exempt while you're living there, and there are protections if your spouse continues to live there. However, Medicaid can place a lien on your home and seek recovery after your death. Proper planning can help protect your home for your heirs.

Can I give away my assets to qualify for Medicaid?

Simply giving away assets can result in penalties and may not be the best strategy. However, there are legal planning techniques that can help protect assets while maintaining Medicaid eligibility. Working with an experienced elder law attorney is essential to navigate these complex rules.

What happens to my spouse if I need Medicaid?

Ohio has protections to prevent spousal impoverishment. The "community spouse" can keep certain assets and income while the other spouse receives Medicaid benefits. We can help maximize what your spouse retains while still qualifying for benefits.

Estate Planning

Do I really need a will if I don't have many assets?

Yes! A will isn't just about distributing assets—it also names guardians for minor children, designates who handles your affairs, and ensures your wishes are followed. Without a will, Ohio law determines who gets your assets, which may not match your wishes.

What's the difference between a will and a trust?

A will goes through probate court after your death, which is public and can take months. A trust avoids probate, keeps your affairs private, and can provide more control over how and when assets are distributed. Trusts can also help with incapacity planning during your lifetime.

How often should I update my estate plan?

Review your estate plan every 3-5 years or after major life events: marriage, divorce, births, deaths, significant asset changes, or moves to a new state. Laws also change, so periodic reviews ensure your plan remains effective.

VA Benefits

Do I have to have served in combat to qualify for VA benefits?

No. For Aid & Attendance benefits, you need 90 days of active duty with at least one day during a wartime period, but you don't have to have served in combat or even overseas. Many veterans don't realize they qualify.

Can I receive both VA benefits and Medicaid?

In some cases, yes. VA benefits and Medicaid have different eligibility rules, and receiving one doesn't automatically disqualify you from the other. However, coordination is important to maximize benefits from both programs.
